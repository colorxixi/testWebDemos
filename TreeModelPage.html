Many scientists around the world are making efforts to monitor the status and the change of the trees that may determine our future life. I just tried to grab a droplet of the sea by applying remote sensing technology to forestry. I will start from parametrizing forest trees with biophysical variables, touch on the current methods to retrieve biophysical variables and then raise my own points of interest.

To wake up, I'd like to share this demo1 first. We can notice there is a bunch of yellow particles being emitted at certain time interval. I intend to use this particles to represent the photon rays from the sum. When a photon reaches the surface of an object (atmosphere/vegetation/ground or whatever), it has a probability of becoming absorbed, reflected or transmitted. The reflectance can be specular or diffuse. My demo just simulates the behavior of specular reflectance, diffuse reflectance and transmission, without consider absorption. The outgoing reflectance can be captured by the remote sensing instrument and can be further processed into product.

When you move the objects(i.e. the branches and sphere) to a different location, the reflectance behavior correspondingly differs. Although I did not add the leaves to the trees, I just want to use this very simple demonstration to show that, the structure of trees will affect the reflectance behavior to a great degree. Changing the geometry of sun light incident angle, the tree structure and any other settings will change the directional reflectance. From this perspective, it is possible to model the relationship between the forest information and the remote sensing product.

Forest information? How can I get?

There are plenty of forest parameters to take care of. Physically: species, development stage, coverage, height, crown closure, crown size, crown depth, stem density, basal area, Diameter breast height (DBH), leaf area index (LAI), crown/leaf clumping index, fraction of photosynthetically active radiation (FPAR), biomass, Net primary productivity, age, height of the first live branch, subcanopy/understory diversity, etc. Chemically: chlorophyll content, water content, leaf nitrogen, chlorophyll fluorescence, fire fuel, etc.

These physical/chemical variables can be measured in certain ways which represent the change and status of forest. The question is that field samples of these variables are usually very limited. A promising solution is to infer these variables continuously from the existing remote sensing products with broad temporal and spatial coverage. Generally, statistical models or physical models are built to estimate the unknown values of forest variables from the known. Various statistical models, e.g. regression, and statistical learning models, provide fast and effective means of such estimation as long as forest samples are sufficient. In parallel to the statistical models, the so-called physical models, stem from physical principles such as radiosity, radiative transfer, geometrical optics, or computer ray tracing. In their first step, these sophisticated models usually quantify a scene presumably composed of trees and the ground with the physical/chemical parameters like LAI, leaf angle, chlorophyll, etc. The spectral property such as reflectance and transmission behavior of a scene unit is also defined based on a-priori knowledge. The aforementioned physical principles are then constructed in the form of equations or recursions, which tie the physical/chemical variables and remotely sensed radiations together. The construction is usually in the forward direction from the radiation source to the receptors. Our target process is actually in the inverse direction so that we can get physical/chemical variables from the remotely sensed reflectance. In this situation, the statistical models can be useful to model this inverse process. The inversion results are the forest physical/chemical variables. Despite the complexity of the physical models, the requirement for forest samples are minimized and validation of a physical model is doable. Studying and applying the physical models has draw constant attention in remote sensing.

Something promising

The demo1 shows a simplified principle of the computer-based simulation models to render an object. Actually, the computer-based simulation models are held to be more accurate than other types of physical models and therefore have great implication for correcting other physical models. One detailed example can be found in [1] .

However, this important linkage between the micro-scale tree characteristics to macro-scale reflectance information has not been self-contained due to the simplicity of the tree geometries and many other factors. The encyclopedic library for diverse tree species seem to be set aside so far. On the other hand, the rapid development of sensing technology, especially airborne and terrestrial LiDAR, has tremendously enabled the field of tree reconstruction into a practical stage. A huge gap is formed. Not too bad, I still find several strong teams are doing this research topics [2] [3] . I am so thrilled at their breakthroughs and I just cannot help trying to throw myself in the air. Computers can offer us details and we cannot ignore this great power.

My point goes here---Branches!

The simplicity of tree geometries such as random distribution of leaves need be revised based on the integration of point clouds and radiance measurements. The tree geometries requires covering more accurate tree distribution functions, leaf distribution functions, dynamics of leaves and flowers, orientations of leaves, bare trunk influence. In fact, leaves grow under strong constraint of their branches. The count, alignment and orientation of leaves and their growing pattern are definitely impacted by the branch spatial properties. These rules can be observed and stored as prior information database with species as a key. Since it is possible to have species classifications in a 3D scene based on surveys or any other techniques as well as the rich spatial and spectral information, we can reconstruct the real forest scene with the aid of computer algorithms. The results are tree structure database and we can obtain the real statistics for the metrics that we are interested in.

At least three contributions can be made by such research:
1) Providing true leaf area for LAI measurement.
    The traditional labor for trapping leaf litters to measure LAI can be minimized. Branch based LAI estimates will effectively address the overlapping effect of leaves.
2) Simulating the dynamics of greening and defoliation more accurately.
    The growing and falling of leaves are not sudden. They keep a certain order to turn dense or sparse.
3) Quantifying the visible tree responses to environmental factors.
    The bias of branch directions is affected by water resource and light hours. More unknown macro metrics of branches that relates to environmental factors are worth research attentions.
4) Exposing animal, fire and other factors that cause forest disturbance.
    The scanning technology achieves amazement by capturing some small-scale objects that used to be invisible.

[-- updated Jan.7.2014] I have added demo3 and demo4 to illustrate the geometric modeling of leaf and scene. Something playable is the simulation of leaf bend factor, leaf angle distribution, leaf and crown position distribution. The two demos do not reflect anything real but does follow the distribution assumptions of some studies about building physical models. These studies also assume that a pixel in a remote sensed image can be decomposed to shadow area, and sunlit area. The total reflectance is a sum of the reflectance from shadow and from sunlit area if the reflected light does not scatter multiple times. The assumptions about distribution and reflectance can apply in large scales, but not in this high resolution case. As seen from the simple demo3 and demo4 , when you try a change of bend factor, the shadow will change drastically and the calculation of shadow area will be challenged.

In essence, the reflectance can be more accurately calculated using the ray tracing technology, based on the radiative transfer equation, or called the rendering equation in computer graphics.
\( L_0(p,w_0) = L_e(p,w_0) + \int_{2\pi^+}f_r(p,w_i,w_0)L_0(r_c(p,w_i),-w_i)cos\theta_idw_i \)
Devoid of atmosphere, \( L_0 \) is the outgoing radiance, \( p \) is the ray hit point, \( w_0 \) is the ray outgoing direction, \( L_e \) is the self-emitted radiance from the ray hit point, \( f_r \) is the BRDF, \( w_i \) is the incident ray direction at the hit point, \( r_c \) is the backward ray hit point. The detailed explanation can be found in Suffen 2007 [4].

Combining ray tracing with trunk and leaf geometric modeling, a realistic scene for forest monitoring and ecological analysis is within reach.
